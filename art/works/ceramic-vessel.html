Tea Bowl

stoneware, hare's fur glaze — jian ware

This vessel took three weeks from wedging to unloading. It is one of eight that survived the firing — the kiln decides what it keeps.

niansong zhang

the clay

It begins with the material itself. The stoneware clay is wedged by hand to remove air pockets and align the particles — a meditative, repetitive motion that sets the rhythm for everything that follows.

throwing

Centered on the wheel, the clay opens into a cylinder and then slowly expands outward. The vessel emerges not from a plan but from a conversation between intention and the clay's willingness to move.

trimming

Once leather-hard, the vessel is inverted and the excess clay at the base is carved away. This reveals the foot ring — the quiet detail that gives the piece its stance.

the surface

Still raw, still waiting. The surface before glazing holds the memory of every touch — the trimming tool's concentric marks that will catch the ash glaze later.

glazing

A wood-ash glaze is mixed from the kiln's own residue — a closed loop between fire and surface. The vessel is dipped quickly; the porous bisqueware absorbs the water and leaves behind an even coat of mineral dust that will melt into glass.

the firing

Forty-eight hours in a wood-fired kiln. The temperature climbs past 1280 degrees and the ash carried by the flame settles on the shoulders of each piece, creating unpredictable pools and drips. This is the moment where control is surrendered entirely.

What remains is an object shaped equally by hand and by forces beyond the hand's reach.
